Why doesn’t appendicitis pain radiate from the umbilicus to the right lower quadrant in older patients?
(In typical appendicitis cases seen in younger patients, pain often begins as a vague discomfort around the umbilicus (mediated by T10 visceral afferents) and then localizes to the right lower quadrant (RLQ) as the parietal peritoneum becomes involved. In elderly patients, however, this classic “visceral-to-somatic” pain migration is frequently absent.)

1) Age‐related changes in pain perception

• Decline in sensory receptors and neurons: As we age, there is a documented decrease in tactile and nociceptive sensitivity. Peripheral receptors—those that detect pressure, temperature, and pain—become fewer in number, and blood flow to these receptors diminishes. In particular, sensory neurons in the dorsal root ganglia (DRG) increase through mid‐life but then decline in both size and number. This means that the initial visceral pain signal from the inflamed appendix (carried by unmyelinated C‐fibers) may be weaker or delayed.

• Reduced visceral pain transmission: The appendix’s early inflammatory insult produces a poorly localized, “dull” visceral pain. In older adults, degeneration of spinal afferent fibers further blunts that signal. Even if the appendix is inflamed, fewer or less‐responsive visceral afferents may not transmit enough nociceptive input for the patient to perceive a mid‐abdominal ache around the umbilicus.

• Blurring of visceral vs. somatic pain: The diagnostic value of migratory pain relies on a two‐step process—first, vague visceral discomfort; then, sharp somatic pain when the parietal peritoneum is irritated. With age‐related sensory decline, the transition from visceral to somatic pain can become indistinct. If the visceral component is weak or delayed, or if somatic localization is impaired (due to reduced peripheral sensitivity), the classic pattern never fully emerges. Clinically, this blurring makes it harder to distinguish early appendicitis from other causes of vague abdominal discomfort in an older patient.

2) Immune senescence and blunted inflammatory amplification

• Disruption of neuroimmune signaling: In younger individuals, inflammation in the appendix triggers a cascade—mast cells release histamine, tryptase, and nerve growth factor (NGF); enterochromaffin cells release serotonin (5‐HT)—that amplifies nociceptor sensitivity. These mediators act on TRPV1‐ and Nav1.8‐expressing sensory neurons via PAR‐2 and trkA receptors, creating the “inflammatory soup” needed to convert mild visceral distension into a conscious pain signal.

• Decline in mast cells and NGF⁺ signaling: With aging, mast cell density in the bowel mucosa falls by about 30–40%. This reduces local NGF availability, which is crucial for maintaining healthy sensory nerve endings. Likewise, enterochromaffin cells make fewer contacts with nearby nerves, so 5‐HT–mediated potentiation of pain receptors is diminished.

• Sensory nerve degeneration: Reduced retrograde transport of NGF leads to downregulation of pain‐transducing channels (TRPV1, Nav1.8) on sensory neurons. As a result, even if the appendix is inflamed, the peripheral nerves are less capable of generating a robust signal.

• Net effect on early appendicitis: Because the inflammatory mediators and sensory nerves that normally convert early visceral inflammation into conscious pain are both impaired, elderly patients experience a blunted “inflammatory soup.” Early appendicitis may induce minimal or no mid‐abdominal discomfort, and there is insufficient sensitization of somatic nerves for sharp RLQ pain to develop in the classic way.
